Week of April 1–6, 2026

The Flemish block explodes into life

Dwars door Vlaanderen, Tour of Flanders, and the opening of the Tour of the Basque Country ignite April.

+
🪨

Flemish Classics & the Basque opener

Ganna takes Dwars, Pogačar wins Flanders, and Seixas starts Itzulia in yellow.

Recap

April opened with one of the most dramatic finales of the cobbled spring at Dwars door Vlaanderen (April 1), where Filippo Ganna won in 3:48:27 after catching Wout van Aert in the final meters of the run-in to Waregem, while Søren Wærenskjold completed the podium. The race was 184.6 km long from Roeselare to Waregem, and the reports in the results pages describe Van Aert as having attacked decisively on the Eikenberg, dropped his companions one by one, and looked on course for victory until Ganna reeled him in right at the finish. [domestique...ycling.com], [cyclingstage.com], [procyclingstats.com] [domestique...ycling.com], [cyclingstage.com]

The first Monument of the month followed immediately at the Tour of Flanders (April 5), where Tadej Pogačar won the 278.2 km race from Antwerp to Oudenaarde in 6:20:07, beating Mathieu van der Poel by 34 seconds and Remco Evenepoel by 1:11. Wout van Aert finished fourth and Mads Pedersen fifth, which matters because it meant the biggest cobbled names all featured deep into the finale rather than being eliminated early. [domestique...ycling.com]

At the same time, the Tour of the Basque Country began on April 6 with a Bilbao time trial, and Paul Seixas immediately took both the stage and the race lead. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Rider Analysis

This week produced three distinct narratives. Ganna looked like a rider stretching beyond his time trial identity and into genuine top-tier Classics territory, because Dwars was not just a watts test — it was a WorldTour one-day race won against a field that still included Van Aert, Philipsen, Pedersen, Laporte, and Girmay near the finish. [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Pogačar, meanwhile, delivered the most important result of the week. Winning Flanders ahead of van der Poel and Evenepoel, with Van Aert and Pedersen also in the top five, showed that his cobbled form was not theoretical or opportunistic — it stood up against the full elite field of the discipline. [domestique...ycling.com]

Then there was Seixas, whose Basque opening stage win placed him on a very different storyline. A rider who had already been visible in the spring now moved from “promising” into “controlling a WorldTour stage race.” [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Prediction

From that point, the next question was whether Pogačar’s Flanders form would translate to Paris–Roubaix, where raw power, cobblestone handling, and late-race durability are even more brutal. At the same time, the Basque Country looked set to become a real proving ground for Seixas, because he was not just leading after one day — he was positioned to turn early speed into a full stage-race statement. [en.wikipedia.org], [en.wikipedia.org] [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Week of April 7–13, 2026

Seixas rules the Basque Country, Van Aert conquers Roubaix

Historic Itzulia control and a fastest-ever Paris–Roubaix define the week.

+
🏁

Itzulia mastery & Hell of the North

Seixas turns promise into dominance, while Van Aert captures Roubaix authority.

Recap

The Tour of the Basque Country turned into a breakout week for Paul Seixas. He won Stage 1, Stage 2, and Stage 5, stayed in yellow throughout the race, and took the final overall by 2:30 over Florian Lipowitz and 2:33 over Tobias Halland Johannessen. Stages 3, 4, and 6 went to Axel Laurance, Alex Aranburu, and Andrew August respectively. The official Itzulia site said Seixas became the youngest rider ever to win the Itzulia, which elevates the result from “good stage race” to “historically significant performance.” [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com], [watts2win.eu] [itzulia.eus]

Then came Paris–Roubaix (April 12). The race covered 258.3 km from Compiègne to Roubaix, including 54.8 km of cobblestones across 30 sectors, and Wout van Aert beat Tadej Pogačar in a sprint finish in 5h 16' 52". Jasper Stuyven was third, Mathieu van der Poel fourth, and Christophe Laporte fifth. The race report also records it as the fastest ever edition, at an average speed of 48.91 km/h. [en.wikipedia.org]

Rider Analysis

For Van Aert, Roubaix was not just another big win. The result page states it was his first Paris–Roubaix victory and also the first Belgian men’s win in the race since 2019, which gave the success both personal and national significance. Given how often Van Aert’s Classics campaigns have featured heroic rides without the final prize, winning Roubaix in the fastest edition on record gave him a very different kind of authority. [en.wikipedia.org] [en.wikipedia.org]

For Seixas, the Basque Country was arguably the biggest single breakthrough of the spring. He did not just edge the GC; he controlled the race, won three of the six stages, and finished the week clear of strong stage racers such as Lipowitz and Johannessen. [procyclingstats.com], [watts2win.eu]

Pogačar, although second in Roubaix, actually reinforced his aura rather than diminishing it. Across just over a week he had won Flanders and then finished runner-up in Roubaix — two of the hardest one-day races in the sport, on very different forms of cobbled racing. [domestique...ycling.com], [en.wikipedia.org]

Prediction

With the cobbled Monuments finished, the spotlight shifted toward the Ardennes, where the terrain would favor riders with more punch on steep climbs than in flat, attritional cobbled sectors. Based on the results so far, Evenepoel, Pogačar, and Seixas looked like the most natural names to watch, but the key unknown was whether Seixas could carry his Basque form directly into one-day hill races. [domestique...ycling.com], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclingstage.com], [itzulia.eus]

Evenepoel strikes at the Amstel Gold Race

The cobbles fade and the Ardennes begin with a selective, two-man finish.

⛰️

Amstel opens the Ardennes chapter

Evenepoel edges Skjelmose and stamps a new Ardennes signature.

Recap

The transition from cobbles to Ardennes began with the Amstel Gold Race (April 19), where Remco Evenepoel won the 257.2 km race from Maastricht to Berg en Terblijt in 5:59:40 after a long two-man move with Mattias Skjelmose, whom he beat by one second. Benoît Cosnefroy finished third at 1:59, with Romain Grégoire fourth and Emiel Verstrynge fifth on the same time as Cosnefroy. [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

The supporting race reports describe Amstel as being shaped on climbs such as the Kruisberg, Keutenberg, and the late Cauberg, with Evenepoel and Skjelmose separating themselves from the rest and staying clear to the line. What matters in narrative terms is the time gap: by the finish, the lead duo had nearly two minutes over the next group. [domestique...ycling.com], [cyclingstage.com] [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Rider Analysis

Evenepoel’s Amstel win mattered because it gave him a clean, high-level Ardennes result against another elite all-round puncheur in Skjelmose. It also offered a contrast to his Flanders result the week before: at Flanders he was a very strong podium rider; at Amstel he became the outright race winner. [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com] [domestique...ycling.com], [domestique...ycling.com]

The result also showed Skjelmose remained a major factor in hilly one-day racing. Losing by only one second after almost six hours of racing kept him firmly inside the top Ardennes tier rather than relegating him to supporting-cast status. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Cosnefroy’s third place at nearly two minutes, meanwhile, underlined how selective the race became. He was best of the rest, but the week’s main story was still that Evenepoel and Skjelmose were operating on a clearly higher line of performance in Limburg. [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Prediction

After Amstel, the obvious question was whether Evenepoel’s strength could transfer to La Flèche Wallonne, where the race usually narrows to one brutally steep final climb rather than a rolling sequence of attacks. At the same time, Seixas’ Basque shape made him a natural name for Huy, because riders arriving with climbing rhythm and sharp acceleration often thrive there. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclingnews.com], [cyclinglookout.com]

Week of April 20–26, 2026

Seixas wins Huy, Pogačar finishes the Ardennes with Liège

A breakthrough week for Seixas, but Pogačar remains the reference point.

+
🏔️

Mur de Huy drama & Liège authority

Seixas explodes into the top tier, while Pogačar closes the Ardennes in Monument style.

Recap

The week’s first major result was La Flèche Wallonne (April 22), where Paul Seixas won in 4:35:29, finishing three seconds ahead of Mauro Schmid and Ben Tulett, while Benoît Cosnefroy placed fourth and Mattias Skjelmose fifth. Several reports noted that Seixas became the youngest winner in Flèche Wallonne history, which is the kind of stat that turns a strong result into a landmark result. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclinglookout.com], [domestique...ycling.com]

The Ardennes then closed with Liège–Bastogne–Liège (April 26), where Tadej Pogačar won in 5:50:28, with Paul Seixas second at +0:45 and Remco Evenepoel third at +1:42. The top ten also included Emiel Verstrynge, Egan Bernal, Pello Bilbao, Romain Grégoire, and Tobias Halland Johannessen. The various result pages consistently describe Pogačar and Seixas going clear on La Redoute, before Pogačar finally shook Seixas on Roche-aux-Faucons. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclingstage.com], [cyclingarchives.com]

Rider Analysis

This was the week when Paul Seixas stopped being “the rider who had a great Basque Country” and became a central name in the spring narrative. He won Flèche Wallonne and then finished second at Liège behind Pogačar, which means he backed up a steep-finish classic win with a Monument podium within four days. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com], [procyclingstats.com]

But the week also confirmed that Pogačar still sat on top of the hierarchy. Liège was his fourth win in the race, and the sources note that this drew him level with Alejandro Valverde and Moreno Argentin, leaving only Eddy Merckx ahead on the all-time list. [cyclingarchives.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Evenepoel finished the week with an Amstel win and a Liège podium, which is still a very high Ardennes return, but he was no longer clearly the solitary challenger to Pogačar. By the end of April, Seixas had moved into that conversation too. [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Prediction

With the Ardennes done, the immediate focus moved to Tour de Romandie, which often acts as a bridge between spring one-day form and early-summer stage-race shape. The key question was whether Pogačar would convert Monument-winning legs into multi-day control, and whether in-form riders like Lipowitz and Lenny Martinez could turn consistency into a podium in a shorter WorldTour stage race. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclinggrid.com], [bahraincyc...ngteam.com]

Week of April 27–May 3, 2026

Pogačar crushes Romandie, Zimmermann lands a career win

A WorldTour overmatch in Switzerland and a landmark German victory at Eschborn–Frankfurt.

+
⚡

Romandie domination & a breakthrough one-day win

Pogačar overwhelms the field, while Zimmermann claims a home-roads headline.

Recap

The Tour de Romandie (April 28–May 3) became a showcase for Tadej Pogačar. The stage winners were Dorian Godon in the prologue, then Pogačar on Stages 1, 2, 4, and 5, with Godon also taking Stage 3. Pogačar won the overall in 20:05:42, with Florian Lipowitz second at +0:42 and Lenny Martinez third at +2:44. In plain terms, he won four of the six race days and the general classification. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com], [cyclingstage.com] [procyclingstats.com], [cyclingstage.com]

On May 1, Eschborn–Frankfurt added a different kind of story. Georg Zimmermann won the 211.4 km race in 4:59:34, beating Tom Pidcock and Ben Tulett in a sprint from a late front group. The official race site called it the biggest win of Zimmermann’s career, and the result pages note he became the first German winner since 2019. [eschborn-f...ankfurt.de], [domestique...ycling.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Rider Analysis

Romandie suggested that, at least in short stage-race format, Pogačar was operating at a different level from the rest of the field. Winning GC by 42 seconds is impressive on its own, but doing it while taking four stages makes the hierarchy unambiguous. [cyclinggrid.com], [cyclingstage.com]

Lipowitz deserves attention here as well. Finishing second to Pogačar is not a defeat in the usual sense; it is also evidence that he could survive and respond better than almost everyone else across a compact, high-level route. Lenny Martinez also left Switzerland with a podium and the best young rider classification, preserving his upward trend after the Ardennes block. [cyclinggrid.com], [cyclingstage.com] [bahraincyc...ngteam.com]

Zimmermann’s Eschborn win mattered because it gave the spring a different kind of headline: not dominance from a global superstar, but a career-defining WorldTour win on home roads for a rider who had been in the conversation without owning a signature result of that size. [eschborn-f...ankfurt.de], [domestique...ycling.com]

Prediction

After Romandie, the calendar was about to shift from one-day explosiveness and short stage-race aggression into the three-week logic of the Giro d’Italia, which started on May 8. That meant the story would become less about who could produce one huge climb or one winning sprint and more about who could stay consistent through 21 stages, a Bulgarian start, and nearly 3,460 km of racing. [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclingstats.com] [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclingstats.com]

Week of May 4–10, 2026

The Giro begins in Bulgaria

Fast openers, jersey volatility, and the first hints of how the race may evolve.

+
🇮🇹

Grand Tour opening block

Magnier strikes twice, Silva takes a day, and the Giro begins to build its shape.

Recap

The 2026 Giro d’Italia started on May 8 in Nessebar, Bulgaria, and the race would run through 21 stages before finishing in Rome on May 31. The first three stages produced a fast, nervous opening block: Paul Magnier won Stage 1, Guillermo Thomas Silva won Stage 2, and Magnier again won Stage 3. After those three days, Magnier had two stage wins, but the race lead had already changed hands. [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclingstats.com] [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

Rider Analysis

The first weekend of the Giro belonged to fast finishers and opportunists rather than the biggest general classification names. Magnier’s two wins immediately made him a major figure in the points competition, while Silva’s stage and time in the leader’s jersey showed how volatile the opening days were. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

That volatility matters. In Grand Tours, the first days often look simple on paper but are crucial because they create pressure, jersey obligations, and early time losses that can shape team behavior later. Even without a major GC mountain stage yet, the race was already becoming structured around who had responsibilities and who could hide. [procyclingstats.com], [en.wikipedia.org]

Prediction

The first mountain tests were always likely to change the tone of the Giro. The route list shows that Stage 7 and Stage 9 were mountain stages, and those looked like the first days that could really bring the overall favorites into direct conflict. [procyclingstats.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Week of May 11–17, 2026

The Giro finds its first real shape

Eulálio becomes a surprise jersey story, while Vingegaard begins to define the mountains.

+
📈

From volatility to emerging race logic

Stage wins spread around, but the general classification starts to clarify.

Recap

The second Giro week began after the first rest day and immediately broadened the race narrative. Jhonatan Narváez won Stage 4, Igor Arrieta won Stage 5, and Davide Ballerini won Stage 6. After Stage 5, Afonso Eulálio moved into the overall lead and retained it through Stages 6, 7, 8, and 9. [procyclingstats.com]

The mountains then started to leave a clearer imprint. Jonas Vingegaard won Stage 7 and Stage 9, while Narváez also added Stage 8, giving him two stage wins in the race by that point. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

Rider Analysis

This was the point at which the Giro turned from “open early race” into “race with an emerging logic.” Eulálio holding the lead through multiple stages matters because it meant the pink jersey was not immediately monopolized by the pre-race favorite. At the same time, Vingegaard’s two mountain-stage wins in one week were a very clear signal that, even without the race lead yet, he was shaping the hardest terrain. [procyclingstats.com] [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

Narváez’s second stage win also deserves emphasis because it made him one of the race’s most visible aggressive riders of the first half, not just a one-day opportunist inside a Grand Tour. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

Prediction

The route moved next into a sequence that included the 42 km time trial on Stage 10 and then a series of transitional but increasingly selective stages. That meant the next week would likely show whether Eulálio could still defend pink against stronger, more established GC specialists — above all Vingegaard. [procyclingstats.com], [procyclingstats.com] [procyclingstats.com], [en.wikipedia.org]

Week of May 18–24, 2026

Vingegaard takes control

The Giro’s decisive hierarchy change arrives on Stage 14.

+
👑

The turning point of the Giro

Varied stage winners continue, but the maglia rosa finds its long-term owner.

Recap

This was the turning-point week of the Giro. After the second rest day, Filippo Ganna won the Stage 10 time trial, while Narváez, Alec Segaert, and Alberto Bettiol won Stages 11, 12, and 13. But the crucial race moment came on Stage 14, when Jonas Vingegaard won again and finally took the overall lead. Fredrik Dversnes Lavik then won Stage 15, but from that point onward the maglia rosa was on Vingegaard’s shoulders. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com] [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com]

Rider Analysis

The first half of the race had multiple leaders, multiple stage winners, and a much wider sense of volatility. This week ended that phase. The Giro Wikipedia summary states that Vingegaard took the lead on Stage 14 and then slowly extended his lead for the rest of the race. That matters because it means Stage 14 was not just another mountain win — it was the decisive change in hierarchy. [en.wikipedia.org] [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclingstats.com]

There was also significance in how many different rider profiles kept winning stages around him. Ganna’s time trial win, Bettiol’s long solo, Segaert’s success, and Narváez’s repeated raids all showed the Giro was lively and varied even as the GC itself became less open. [procyclingstats.com], [cyclinggrid.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Prediction

Once Vingegaard had taken pink on Stage 14, the final week looked less like a battle to discover the strongest rider and more like a battle to measure how dominant he could be, and who would survive around him for the podium. [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclinguk.com]

Week of May 25–31, 2026

Vingegaard closes the Giro in dominant fashion

A structurally dominant Grand Tour win sets up the sport’s next major rivalry.

+
🏆

Rome closes a landmark Giro

Five stage wins, all-three-Grand-Tours history, and a shifting Tour de France narrative.

Recap

The final week brought the decisive closing sequence: Vingegaard won Stage 16, Michael Valgren won Stage 17, Paul Magnier won Stage 18, Sepp Kuss won Stage 19, Vingegaard won Stage 20, and Jonathan Milan won the final stage in Rome. [procyclingstats.com], [procyclingstats.com], [bikeworldnews.com]

By the end of the race, Jonas Vingegaard had won the overall in 83:22:51, with Felix Gall second at +5:22 and Jai Hindley third at +6:25. The Giro summary also notes that this made Vingegaard the eighth rider to win all three Grand Tours, and the first since Chris Froome in 2018. [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclingstats.com], [bikeworldnews.com]

The secondary classifications also matter in understanding the final shape of the race: Paul Magnier won the points classification, Giulio Ciccone won the mountains classification, Afonso Eulálio won the young rider classification, and Visma–Lease a Bike took the team classification. [en.wikipedia.org], [bikeworldnews.com]

Rider Analysis

The broadest conclusion is simple: this was Vingegaard’s race. The official summary says he won five stages, with four of them in the mountains, and after Stage 14 he steadily increased his lead. That is not a squeaked-out Grand Tour; it is a structurally dominant one. [en.wikipedia.org] [en.wikipedia.org], [procyclinguk.com]

Felix Gall was arguably the biggest supporting success story. He finished second overall, and the final classification recap describes it as the best Grand Tour of his career. Hindley taking third completed a podium of proven climbers, while Eulálio’s sixth overall and white jersey marked one of the real breakout performances of the race. [bikeworldnews.com], [procyclinguk.com] [procyclinguk.com], [en.wikipedia.org]

The final week also strengthened Visma’s internal narrative heading toward summer. Kuss winning Stage 19 and Vingegaard taking Stages 16 and 20 meant the team did not just defend the race — it actively authored the final mountain sequence. [procyclingstats.com], [procyclingstats.com]

Prediction

Once the Giro ended, the next natural storyline was not just “who wins the Tour,” but how Vingegaard’s Giro authority would alter the shape of the season’s next rivalry with Pogačar, who had already dominated so much of the spring in one-day racing and shorter stage races. If April had belonged mainly to Pogačar and Seixas, May ended with Vingegaard reclaiming the stage-race center of gravity. [en.wikipedia.org], [cyclingstage.com] [cyclingstage.com], [en.wikipedia.org]
